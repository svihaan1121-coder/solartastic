Earth Information

1. About the Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun, about 4.5 billion years old, and the only astronomical object known to harbor life. This is largely due to its position in the circumstellar habitable zone, also referred to as the Goldilocks zone, where liquid water can exist on a planet's surface.

Structurally, Earth is composed of four distinct layers: a solid inner core of iron and nickel, a liquid outer core, a highly viscous mantle, and a thin outer crust. The movement of tectonic plates in the crust, driven by convection in the mantle, leads to continuous geological shifts, causing earthquakes, forming mountains, and generating volcanic activity.

2. Biosphere & Water Coverage

Approximately 71% of Earth's surface is covered by liquid water, mostly contained in its vast oceans, including the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Southern, and Arctic oceans. These oceans play an essential role in regulating global climate patterns and maintaining the chemical balance necessary for biological ecosystems.

The biosphere, representing the global ecological system integrating all living beings, features massive biodiversity. Ecosystems extend from microscopic bacterial networks deep in the soil to tropical rainforests and marine habitats, sustaining millions of plant and animal species, including human civilizations.

3. Atmosphere & Shielding

The atmosphere of Earth is composed of approximately 78% Nitrogen, 21% Oxygen, and trace amounts of other gases, including argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapor. This composition provides breathable air and insulates the surface against solar temperatures via the greenhouse effect.

Importantly, the ozone layer situated in the stratosphere filters out harmful ultraviolet radiation from the sun, making terrestrial life possible. Additionally, the gaseous layers cushion the planet from meteoroid impacts, vaporizing most small debris before it reaches the ground.

4. Core & Magnetosphere

Driven by currents of molten iron in the outer core, Earth generates a powerful magnetic field known as the magnetosphere. This magnetic envelope extends into space, shielding the planet's atmosphere and surface from cosmic rays and solar winds, preventing solar radiation from stripping away life-supporting atmosphere.
